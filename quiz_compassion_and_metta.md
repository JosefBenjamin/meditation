# Quiz: On Compassion and Loving-Kindness (Metta)

**Q1 · On self-compassion** — *Why do many traditions suggest offering loving-kindness to oneself before offering it to others?*
- ✓ Because extending genuine warmth to others is difficult if the heart is closed or harsh toward oneself.
- · Because you are the only person who truly matters in your practice.
- · Because it is the easiest step and builds confidence quickly.
- · Because offering it to others first will deplete your energy.
- *Gentle*: The heart is a vessel. We cannot pour out what we have not allowed ourselves to receive. Kindness to the self is the wellspring of compassion for the world.

**Q2 · On difficult people** — *When offering metta to someone who has caused frustration, what is the intention?*
- · To excuse or justify their harmful behavior.
- · To force yourself to feel deep affection for them.
- ✓ To untangle your own heart from the pain of resentment, recognizing their shared human vulnerability.
- · To prove to yourself that you are a better, more forgiving person than they are.
- *Gentle*: Compassion for a difficult person is not condoning their actions. It is a boundary we set against our own bitterness, allowing us to see clearly without the fog of hatred.

**Q3 · On feelings of emptiness** — *You repeat the phrases of loving-kindness ("May I be happy..."), but feel completely numb or empty. What is true?*
- · You are doing the practice incorrectly and should stop.
- · You are incapable of feeling compassion.
- ✓ The practice is still working; you are planting seeds, even if you cannot feel the weather today.
- · You must force an emotion to arise, or the words are meaningless.
- *Gentle*: The intention is the practice, not the emotion. Sometimes the ground is dry, but the watering itself is an act of faith that softening will eventually come.

**Q4 · On the nature of compassion** — *How does compassion differ from pity?*
- · Pity is stronger and more effective than compassion.
- · Compassion only focuses on the positive, while pity focuses on the negative.
- ✓ Pity creates distance and a sense of superiority, while compassion recognizes our shared, equal humanity in suffering.
- · Pity requires action, while compassion is only a passive feeling.
- *Gentle*: Pity looks down from above. Compassion sits beside. It is the quiet acknowledgment that "your pain could be my pain."

**Q5 · On daily interactions** — *How can the spirit of loving-kindness be practiced outside of formal sitting?*
- · By never disagreeing with anyone or causing any conflict.
- · By ignoring your own needs to constantly serve others.
- ✓ By offering silent wishes of well-being to people you pass in the street or encounter in daily tasks.
- · By demanding that everyone treat you with perfect kindness in return.
- *Gentle*: A silent "may you be at ease" offered to a cashier or a driver in traffic is a profound, invisible thread connecting you to the world.

**Q6 · On boundaries** — *Does practicing compassion mean we must accept harmful behavior from others?*
- · Yes, because setting boundaries is a form of anger and resistance.
- ✓ No, fierce compassion can say a clear "no" to harm, acting to protect oneself or others while still wishing for the perpetrator's ultimate healing.
- · No, but only if you have first tried to change the other person's behavior.
- · Yes, because avoiding conflict is the highest goal of the practice.
- *Gentle*: A boundary can be an act of deep love. We can refuse to participate in harm while still holding a quiet hope that the one causing harm finds their way to peace.
